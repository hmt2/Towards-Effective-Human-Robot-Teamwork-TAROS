A Workshop at TAROS 2026

Towards Effective Human–Robot Teamwork

Exploring how Human–Robot teams are designed and implemented in real-world scenarios — across aerospace, nuclear, autonomous systems, and healthcare.

9 September 2026 · Nancy Rothwell Building, The University of Manchester

Submit a Poster View Schedule

Overview

This workshop explores how Human–Robot teams are designed and implemented in real-world scenarios. The workshop will include invited talks and poster presentations from diverse sectors, including aerospace, nuclear, autonomous systems, and healthcare. It aims to identify transferable principles and foster discussion on designing safe and effective Human–Robot teamwork interactions.

Since Human–Robot teamwork is an emerging application of autonomy, one of our key objectives is to assemble a list of use cases in Human–Robot teaming that provide a foundation for designing Human–Robot teams. Insights from the workshop will feed into a whitepaper discussing different teamwork scenarios to guide practitioners and researchers alike, which we intend to submit to ACM Transactions on Human-Robot Interaction.

Format

A half-day workshop combining invited talks and poster sessions with corresponding lightning talks, followed by a panel discussion representing different application domains. Panel members will include the invited speaker(s) and invited participants from diverse domains.

Call for Posters

We invite poster contributions on topics including, but not limited to:

  • Requirements engineering for Human–Robot interaction and teaming
  • Evaluation methodologies for Human–Robot teamwork systems
  • Deployment challenges and lessons learned
  • Formal specification and verification of Human–Robot teams
  • Case studies involving Human–Robot teams

Organisers

Hazel Taylor

University of Manchester

Hazel is a Post-Doctoral Research Associate at the University of Manchester. She holds funding from VeTSS for “Formalised Software Requirements for Human-Robot Teamwork”. She received her PhD from The University of Manchester for her work on “Enhancing Human-Robot Interaction in Nuclear Environments: Explainability Requirements for Autonomous Systems”. Her research interests include requirements formalisation for Human–Robot teams, practical applications of Human–Robot Interaction, and frameworks to support explainability in critical sectors.

Marie Farrell

University of Manchester

Marie holds a Royal Academy of Engineering Research Fellowship where she is devising new ways of describing, analysing, and assuring the autonomous behaviour of robotic space systems. She held postdoctoral positions at the University of Liverpool and the University of Manchester (on the UKRI-funded FAIR-SPACE Hub), as well as at Maynooth University (on the EU-funded VALU3S project). Her research interests include formal methods, requirements engineering, category theory and its use in Computer Science, critical systems, and assurance. She was secretary of the working group that developed the IEEE 7009 Standard on Fail-Safe Design for Autonomous Systems.

Yasmeen Rafiq

University of Manchester

Yasmeen is a Postdoctoral Research Associate at the University of Manchester working on the CRADLE project in the area of assurance for autonomous and robotic systems. She holds a PhD from the University of York, where her research focused on online Markov chain learning and continual verification for adaptive systems. Her research centres on continuous end-to-end assurance for autonomous systems, bridging the gap between hazard analysis, requirements engineering, and formal modelling. She develops systematic workflows that translate safety requirements into verifiable models using formal methods and tools such as RoboChart, RoboCert, and model checking. Her work has been applied to real-world case studies including robot-assisted dressing and autonomous inspection systems, to enable scalable, verifiable, and safe human-robot interaction.
